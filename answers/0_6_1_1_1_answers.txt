The primary focus of the ISC is to provide Indigenous students with programs and services that support their cultural, emotional, physical, spiritual, and academic well-being, helping them succeed and thrive with a balance of culture, tradition, and academic success.

2.
Our vision is for Indigenous students to succeed and thrive, as our Ancestors envisioned, with a balance of culture, tradition, and academic success.

3.
The ISC supports the well-being of Indigenous students through mental wellness and care services such as ISC Counselling, Elders Program, and Wellness Program. It provides a welcoming space with common areas, study spaces, smudging and medicine, a student kitchen, and a computer lab. The ISC also offers access to additional supports like academic success services, health and counselling, and pathways to other student services.

15.
The ISC logo was designed by Sk’aal Tsiid (Flicker Bird) James Cowpar, Tsaalth Clan, Haida Gwaii. It incorporates cultural elements representing Métis and Plains peoples through the unity and sweet grass, main crests of coastal peoples such as the eagle and raven, and symbols of power, strength, wisdom, and community. The design also features a sacred circle with a man and woman holding a textbook, symbolizing Indigenous identity, unity, and strength.

16.
The symbols in the ISC logo include the eagle, raven, man, woman, and representations of Métis, Plains, and Northwest Coast design/crests. They signify power, strength, wisdom, community, unity, strength among Indigenous peoples, and the diversity of Indigenous communities attending post-secondary institutions. The man and woman holding a textbook represent Indigenous identity and education within a sacred circle.

21.
The ISC incorporates Indigenous identity into its branding through its logo, which features symbols representing Métis, Plains, and Northwest Coast peoples, such as the eagle and raven, along with a West Coast style design of a man and woman holding a textbook within a sacred circle. This logo conveys unity, strength, and the life cycle of an Indigenous student, blending cultural elements to create a sense of identity and community. Additionally, the programs and services emphasize cultural, spiritual, and traditional aspects, supporting Indigenous students' well-being, cultural practices like smudging and medicine, and fostering a social and cultural community through mentorship, workshops, events, and representation in spaces across campuses.
