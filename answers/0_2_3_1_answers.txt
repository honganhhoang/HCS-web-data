2.
The availability of hormone therapy options can depend on where you live and your primary care provider(s) due to the variability in access to trans-specific care, referrals to endocrinologists, and willingness of primary care providers to assess hormone readiness.

3.
- Visit Speaking with Your Provider about Gender-Affirming Care for tips.
- Be open and honest about your feelings and needs.
- Ask questions and seek clarification if needed.
- Advocate for yourself and your healthcare needs.
- Seek support from trusted friends, family members, or healthcare professionals.

4.
- A hormone readiness assessment is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional to determine if a patient is ready to begin hormone therapy.
- It is necessary before starting hormone therapy to ensure that the patient is mentally and physically prepared for the treatment and to assess any potential risks or concerns.

5.
- A primary care provider can refer someone to an endocrinologist for hormone therapy by assessing the individual's readiness for treatment and then making the referral to the endocrinologist.
- The endocrinologist will require a hormone readiness assessment completed by another clinician, such as a mental health clinician, before starting treatment.
- It is important to ask the endocrinologist which providers they accept hormone readiness assessment letters from before starting the assessment process.

6.
Yes, there are clinics or providers in B.C. that offer trans-specific care for hormone therapy.

7.
- A mental health professional provides a readiness assessment for hormone therapy to determine if a patient is ready to begin treatment.
- The assessment period length depends on the health care provider, clinic protocols, and the patient's needs.
- Mental health concerns such as depression or anxiety do not necessarily disqualify someone from hormone therapy, but they may need to be managed before or during treatment.
- Counselling may be recommended or required in some cases, but it is not typically required to get a prescription for hormones.
- Health risks are associated with buying or borrowing hormone medications without a prescription, as the quality and dosage may be unknown and unsafe.

8.
The WPATH Standards of Care are guidelines created by the World Professional Association for Transgender Health to help health care providers offer quality care for transgender and gender-diverse individuals. These standards outline best practices for diagnosis, treatment, and support with the aim of ensuring respectful and affirming health care experiences. The WPATH Standards of Care outline criteria for hormone therapy eligibility for both adults and adolescents. The assessor will confirm if the individual meets these criteria before starting hormone therapy.

9.
For adults:
- Persistent, well-documented gender dysphoria
- Capacity to make a fully informed decision
- Age of majority
- If significant medical or mental health concerns are present, they must be reasonably well-controlled

For adolescents:
- Meet the criteria for the onset of puberty
- Have demonstrated capacity to make a fully informed decision
- Have parental consent and/or assent from a legal guardian
