1.
September 30th marks the anniversary of children being taken from their homes to residential schools, and it is a day set aside to commemorate the residential school experience, honour survivors and their families, and promote reconciliation.

2.
Orange Shirt Day originated as a grassroots effort in 2013 to raise awareness of residential schools. It was inspired by the story of Phyllis (Jack) Webstad, whose bright orange shirt was taken away upon her arrival at the residential school.

3.
The orange shirt symbolizes Phyllis (Jack) Webstad’s bright and excited feelings when she received it, which was taken away upon her arrival at residential school, representing the loss experienced by survivors and their families.

4.
The grassroots effort for Orange Shirt Day began in 2013 in Williams Lake, BC.

19.
The commemoration of Orange Shirt Day is important for future generations because it raises awareness of residential school experiences, promotes reconciliation, and fosters an ongoing commitment to anti-racism, anti-bullying, and healing efforts for Indigenous communities.

20.
Individuals and communities can participate in reconciliation efforts by wearing orange to honor survivors and demonstrate ongoing commitment, attending or organizing awareness events, sharing the story of Phyllis Webstad and the significance of Orange Shirt Day, engaging in educational activities and lesson plans, participating in community gatherings such as drumming and storytelling, and supporting initiatives led by organizations like the ISC, FNSA, or local Indigenous groups.
